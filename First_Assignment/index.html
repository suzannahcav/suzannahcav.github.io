Community Gardens Stand Ground on Liability

This year's celebration of the Latin American stew drew 200 visitors to the Big Bronx Sancochazo.

By Suzannah Cavanaugh

October 7, 2019

On a late September afternoon, Brook Park Community Garden buzzed with neighbors. Program Director Ray Figueroa held court on a central picnic bench.

“This is what community gardens represent,” said Figueroa. He gestured to the 200-person turnout for the Big Bronx Sancochazo. “The potential for organic, grassroots community development happening. That's the real significance of community gardens.”

A new licensing agreement puts that significance at stake.

For 6 months, community gardens and the NYC Parks Department have waged legal battle over restrictive clauses in this year’s license that have left gardens fearful for their future.

Green Thumb, the decades-old division of Parks that oversees community gardens, holds legal control over city gardens via short-term leases.

“The license renewals happen every four years and always have small changes based on experiences from the previous four-year cycle - this cycle is no different,” said Dan Kastanis, Parks’ Press Officer.

New York City’s Community Garden Coalition (NYCCGC) Executive Director Aziz Dehkan disagrees. He said this year’s license contains 20 amended clauses and exercises significantly more control than the previous agreement.

“I think what the city wants to do is cookie cutter and gentrify the community gardens so they’re easier to manage,” said Dehkan.

40 of the 46 community gardens in the South Bronx have not signed the license.

Mike Young, Padre Plaza community garden president, said 40 of the 46 gardens in the South Bronx are hold-outs, fearing liability and termination.

The license jeopardizes Mike Young’s summer workshop: a carpentry program for at-risk high schoolers. If he signs, Young worries about future liability.

“The bottom-line was: if you don’t sign you’ll be locked out of your garden and you will not be permitted on city property. So now we’re getting threatened? That’s nice,” said Young.

Figueroa’s concern is termination. Last winter, he failed to shovel his garden’s surrounding sidewalks. Exhausted, he concentrated on the heavily-trafficked side and hoped the city would recognize his “good faith effort.”

“The next day we were issued a violation from Green Thumb, and I blew a gasket,” said Figueroa.

Figueroa refuses to sign a license that would leave him vulnerable to increased violations, leading to possible termination.

NYCCGC’s legal team continues to negotiate with Parks, bolstered by a city council cohort. Figueroa doesn’t know how long negotiations will take, but he’s confident the remaining gardens won’t capitulate. The hold-outs are adamant about their right to a fair license.
